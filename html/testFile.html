CONs

When exhibiting at trade fairs, there are two important factors to consider in relation to your IP. Can you guess what they are?

    IP rights are territorial. Having a patent, a design, or a trade mark registered in the EU does not mean that you are protected worldwide. Registration is needed in each and every country, especially in South-East Asia where IP infringement is still a major concern.

    Trade fairs are made up of local and foreign exhibitors and visitors. Exhibiting IP owners are disclosing their IP to a much wider audience. For example, if the trade fair is in Singapore but the majority of the visitors are from China, it will be important to explore IP protection in China as well as in Singapore.

GEOGRAPHICAL ISSUES

        Budget will, of course, be a consideration when SMEs decide where to file for IP protection, but it is strongly advised that, if you are unsure, spending a few hundred euros to file a trade mark or a design application (a little more for a patent) is certainly worthwhile.

       First to File system:
IP protection in South-east Asia is based on First to File principle. It means the first party to file an patent/industrial design/trade mark application will own rights to it once the registration is granted.

     Bad Faith Registration: First to File principle, a third party (not the legitimate owner) could utilise the trade fair to obtain and gather the information of your patent, design or trade mark and then immediately file an application to the IP offices to become the legal owner. This may cause various consequences, from losing your IP rights, damaging your reputation or completely excluding you from the market of that country.

TIMING ISSUES

     If you do not have the time to obtain a registration, it is still highly advisable to file an application in the country of interest. Once the IP has been registered, it is then possible to take actions against the infringer. It may also be possible to warn off an infringer by referring to a pending application.

Preparing certification of ownership documents

Leave some time to prepare your documents and have them in order. It is important to have at least a copy of your IP certificates with you at the trade fair. Additionally, all foreign language documents should be translated into the respective language of the country.

Disclosure of my IP – How much is too much?

Throughout the course of a year, you may attend several trade fairs, and at each one, your innovation could be at a different stage of development.

It is crucial to determine whether or not your innovation is ready to be disclosed to the public. Much of this will depend on commercial considerationssuch as whether or not you have a genuinely marketable product.

A too early disclosure may result in others seeing the nascent innovation and improving on it, before claiming it as their own.

Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA): When you find a potential partner and are excited to share your creative works for further negotiation, DO remember to ask the partner to sign in a NDA.

At the moment, it is unlikely that immediate action could be taken at a trade fair, but in some emergency cases , it is possible for IP holders to bring administrative or criminal proceedings at short notice.
Each country in South-East Asia have its own legal remedies , and most countries have a system to search for and seize infringing goods provided that there is very strong evidence of IP infringement.
In many countries such as Thailand, Indonesia or Vietnam, the court, authorities or police may refuse to seize the goods in the case of patent infringement as there may be complex issues involved. Trade marks or copyright infringement are more suitable for such an action.
However, trade fairs usually provide the opportunity for the IP owner to collect evidence of infringement. Such evidence may need to be notarised in order to be admissible in court (e.g. in Vietnam).
Seek an IP lawyer’s advice to examine the evidence and suggest a suitable course of legal action. The IP owner can then decide whether to proceed with an enforcement action or not.

BACKGROUND

A Slovenian Automotive company is exhibiting at the Automotive Trade Fair in Vietnam. Checking the list of exhibitors before starting the exhibition, the company learnt that that a Chinese company, which had been reportedly infringing its design in the past, would also be exhibiting at the same fair.

STEPS TAKEN:

The Slovenian company prepared the original certificate of its design registration in Vietnam and identified a lawyer who could provide advice directly at the exhibition if necessary.

At the trade fair the Chinese competitor was displaying a model of a scooter with an almost identical design to the one owned by the Slovenian company. As advised by the lawyer, the Slovenian company took pictures of the infringing products as well as brochures and other marketing materials.

OUTCOMES:

The Chinese company was served a notification letter during the trade fair and was asked to remove the products infringing the designs owned by the Slovenian company from the exhibition booth. Subsequently, a cease and desist letter was sent and the case proceeded to settlement.

IP LESSIONS:

Prepare in advance: Find out who will be participating at the trade fairs you're attending, identify if potential infringements may take place, and prepare timely copies of your IP certificates

Register your IP in the country where the exhibition takes place and also in the countries where you intend to trade.

Use the help and support of a local lawyer to obtain advice on how to collect valid evidence and what options are available to you.

Soft approaches, such as notification letters and/or cease and desist letters, can be a cost and time effective way to stop an infringement

'CEASE AND DESIST’ LETTER
These are usually done ex parte, i.e. without informing the infringer, in order to take them by surprise. The procedures and relevant authorities are different in each South-East Asian nation, but these raids can usually be organised with two
to three days’ notice. If successful, the infringer will most often be charged with a criminal offence, so there must be evidence of criminal intention (such as counterfeit goods). More common, however, is a civil case, where the infringer has been copying IP but with no criminal intention. Search and seize orders from courts in cases such as these across South-East Asia are rare, but not unheard of. Local legal advice for this type of case is essential.
